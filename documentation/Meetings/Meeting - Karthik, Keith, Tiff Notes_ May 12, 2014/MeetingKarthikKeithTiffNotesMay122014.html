I'd like some discussion of how you plan to configure/design your AngularJS models to sync data to the server. Admittedly, I don't know much about AngularJS, but I imagine there's some configuration involved. Have you looked into this?

In Rails you can define methods on models by putting method definitions in the model's class definition. Just like adding methods to any Ruby class.

Typically controllers don't depend on other controllers. In general, the controller should be a thin veneer (validation, error-handling, responses, etc.) over the meaty business logic stored in utility methods and on models. For example, you might find it difficult just to instantiate a controller instance in order to call its methods. Instead, just defined methods on models and use those.

It's almost certainly not necessary to store the user's password client-side. I have no idea what you'd use that for, and you don't mention it.

You plan to adjust your schedule after some implementation happens--good idea. You should constantly adjust your schedule throughout the rest of the quarter, making sure you're always on-track.

You say you plan hallway usability tests and customer meetings, but I don't see them on your schedule. I see that you plan to do it "while developing", but it would probably be a good idea to schedule this as well to guarantee that you do it and that it's rigorous and structured.

One thing that might help you is the idea of a "stub". For example, you can create the view before any model code ever exists simply by defining a model with all the right fields (or generally right fields) and absolutely no behavior whatsoever. When the real model is finished, you simply swap out the stubbed one and update the view a little to match the real model's interface. This makes it easy to write the bill/task display before the creation, for example.
